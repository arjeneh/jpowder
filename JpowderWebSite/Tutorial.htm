Powder Diffraction and Bragg's Law

Neutron diffraction is used to determine the atomic structure of a material by neutron scattering. A high intensity beam of neutrons is fired at a material, and when it hits the material the neutrons scatter. As physical objects have wave-like properties at the atomic level, the neutrons diffract. The diffracted neutrons are then recognised by sensors that record their position and then the atomic arrangement of the material can be determined. Ideally this process is done with a crystal structure, but when this cannot be done, a powder form of the material is used as a sample. When powder is used, the neutrons are scattered a lot more and create a pattern of rings with the sensors.
[http://code.google.com/p/jpowder/source/browse/wiki/pictures/bowl-stem.jpg]
The JPowder software shows these rings in a graph with the angle of the ring on the x axis and intensity of the ring on the y axis.
